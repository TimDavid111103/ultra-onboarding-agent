Like, what's a real problem you've been handed, what did you build or write to tackle it, and has any of that work produced something concrete a result, a finding, maybe even something heading toward a paper?

---

**Jason:** The day-to-day has shifted a lot over the 1.5 years, which is honestly the part I'm most proud of. Early on I was mostly running existing pipelines, cleaning datasets, sanity-checking outputs, the kind of work that's unglamorous but teaches you how research actually functions. Over time I got handed more independent pieces, and the most substantial one has been building modules to represent transcription factor binding dynamics in our gene regulatory network models, specifically trying to capture how binding affinity changes under different cellular stress conditions in a way that's both biologically accurate and computationally tractable. The concrete challenge was that existing models were either too coarse to capture the dynamics we cared about or too expensive to run at the scale the lab needed, so I spent a few months iterating on a sparse graph-based representation in Python using NumPy and some custom PyTorch layers that could learn binding weights from experimental data. We've gotten results that are promising enough that one of the PhD students I work with has folded my module into a larger manuscript they're currently writing up, so there's a real chance my name ends up on something published, which still feels a little surreal to say out loud.

I want to shift gears just a little and talk about Science Olympiad, because being a captain and reaching state as a team is a different kind of achievement than individual research. What did leading that team actually look like for you? And is there a moment from that experience a win, a failure, a tough conversation that you think shaped how you operate?

---

**Jason:** Leading Science Olympiad was genuinely the hardest thing I did in high school, in a different way than research is hard. Research is mostly you and a problem, but captaining a team means you're responsible for how 15 other people are feeling and performing, and those are much harder variables to control. The moment that shaped me most was actually a failure at regionals junior year, where we came in third when we'd been favored to advance, and the reason wasn't preparation, it was that I'd distributed events based on who I thought was strongest on paper rather than actually talking to people about where they felt confident. Two of our members were placed in events they'd been quietly anxious about for weeks and I hadn't caught it because I wasn't asking the right questions. After that I completely changed how I ran the team, more individual check-ins, more honest conversations about where people actually were versus where I assumed they were, and we made it to state the following year. I think about that failure a lot actually, even in lab work, because there's a version of the same mistake you can make in research, assuming you understand what a collaborator needs or where a model is breaking down without actually interrogating it carefully. The lesson I took from Science Olympiad was that paying close attention to what's actually in front of you, whether that's a teammate or a dataset, matters more than the assumptions you walk in with.

I want to stay with you as a person for one more moment before we move forward. You're clearly someone who goes very deep on hard problems, and that takes a particular kind of mental stamina. When you hit a wall on something a model that won't converge, a concept you can't crack, a week where nothing is working what does that actually look like for you, and how do you get yourself unstuck?

---

**Jason:** Honestly, my first instinct when something isn't working is to keep pushing, which I've learned is actually my worst instinct. When a model won't converge or a proof isn't closing, the version of me from two years ago would just stare at it harder, and that almost never helped. What I've figured out is that getting unstuck usually requires changing the level of abstraction I'm working at. If I'm buried in implementation details, I'll pull back and restate the problem in plain language or on paper, almost like I'm explaining it to someone who doesn't know the codebase, and about half the time the bug or the flawed assumption surfaces immediately just from doing that. The other thing I've learned, mostly from the lab, is that being stuck for more than a day on something usually means I'm missing a piece of context that someone else already has, and the fastest path forward is just asking, which sounds obvious but took me longer to internalize than I'd like to admit because there's a pride thing you have to get over. The hardest wall I hit was about eight months into the Iyengar Lab work when my binding dynamics module kept producing biologically nonsensical outputs and I couldn't figure out why for almost two weeks. It turned out I had a subtle misunderstanding of how cooperativity works in transcription factor binding, a biology gap rather than a code gap, and the fix came from a 20-minute conversation with a postdoc, not from another week of debugging. That experience recalibrated how I think about the boundary between what's a me-problem and what's a go-talk-to-someone problem.

---

The two-week debugging story is a perfect illustration of intellectual humility in action, and the fact that you can name the specific misconception cooperativity in transcription factor binding tells me you actually absorbed the lesson rather than just moving on. **Jason:** This one actually makes me laugh a little because the honest answer is that I got really into fermentation about a year and a half ago, like making kimchi and miso and sourdough, and it started as a purely practical thing because my mom asked me to help in the kitchen. I also think there's something I genuinely value about the fact that humans have been doing this for thousands of years without understanding the mechanism, and now I get to do it while also understanding what's happening at the microbial level, which makes it feel like a small personal connection between the ancient and the modern that I find kind of beautiful.
